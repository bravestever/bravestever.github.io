Skerple Friday: My Baby Skerps the Morning Tape

By Katt | September 10, 2010

Bonus Cat has many ways to communicate that it’s time for breakfast. There’s the direct way, which involves hitting you in the face. The friendly/manipulative method of sitting next to you, purring, and sprinting off to the kitchen every time you shift your weight. The threat: chewing your scalp until you wake up, because you too are made of food.

And then there’s the “I’m so starved I could eat tape” gambit.

He’ll find something with tape attached and start chewing. I don’t know why he considers tape nootrishus-n-tastee. Maybe he’s just doing it to get our attention and rouse pity. Tape is loud.

Skerple Friday: Skerpy Puff

By Katt | September 3, 2010

I was sick last week, and I’ve been having some trouble getting quality sleep this week, and I know I know NO EXCUSES, but it’s been a little tough to find the energy to draw or blog. I’m going on vacation this weekend, though! I’ll be able to practice drawing people in the airport. And the person sitting next to me on the plane will be like “ooh, you’re an artist? whatcha drawing?” and I’ll be all WHAT’S IT TO YA because I’m an introvert and pretty bad at socializing. I would like to preemptively apologize to the person sitting next to me on the plane.

When I first moved to this town, I kept seeing a probably-feral cat sleeping in the lot next door. I tried to be his friend, but feral cats are about as friendly toward humans as I am to my hypothetical airplane seatmate.

He was all white and longhaired, which seems like the worst possible fur choice for a cat who lives on the street, except in winter. He had that kinda ugly feral cat face – you know what I’m talking about? Sort of a longer face with squinty eyes, less open and kittenish, more like a perpetually-irritated lion. And boy was he dirty. I started calling him Dirrty Puff.

Our cat, Bonus Cat, used to be a friend of his. One day I was looking out the window at Dirrty’s usual spot, and there was a bonus cat hanging out with him, hence the name.

The months went on. Bonus became our friend, but Dirrty decided to relocate operations so he wouldn’t be bothered by me. I stopped seeing him regularly, and then I stopped seeing him altogether. I saw him once that summer, and then I saw him in January, and that was it. I feared he’d died at some point during the winter, because he didn’t seem like a healthy cat to begin with (Bonus was cleaner and had much more energy, but it still took four weeks of medication to get him well enough to be neutered). I was sad about that, but you can’t help someone who doesn’t want to be helped.

Anyway, I was walking home two days ago, and I SAW DIRRTY PUFF. He was walking up a porch, looked at me, and ran off across the street in true Puff fashion. Same face and everything, but he looked a lot cleaner. Maybe he has somewhere to stay?

I tried to capture his face here, but it’s not right at all. Cats are hard enough to draw, more so from memory.

Derping on the Derp Side of the Derp

By Katt | August 30, 2010

I mentioned in a previous entry that I am reading Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain, and giving all of the exercises an honest effort. At the time of this posting, I’m about halfway through. I won’t blog a complete blow-by-blow, but I’ll post some highlights and my thoughts.

The first thing you’re supposed to do is a few “before” drawings in pencil. One of them is a self-portrait.

At this point, I’m tempted to skip through to the end and see if all we’re doing is drawing pencil portraits, because I’m really not a pencil-portrait kind of person. I think I got rid of all my drawing pencils years ago, even.

It took me a while to dig up a pencil of any variety, and was thrilled to find a 4B. I’m already ahead of you, book! Finding a pencil sharpener took another five minutes, and even then all I could rustle up was one intended for eyeliner pencils. Eyeliner pencils aren’t intended to be sharpened to a fine point. That’s okay. I don’t need a fine point. I’m an artist.

So here goes.

I’m hesitant to put my face online, so I’m only showing part of the drawing. Enough so you can tell I’m not a horrible artist, but not enough so you can identify me. Which may be beside the point, because my boyfriend says it doesn’t really look like me anyway. My eyes are crooked in this drawing, and I knew it, but I didn’t have an eraser.

Next you are supposed to draw a person from memory. I drew Gordon Ramsay, because we’ve been watching a lot of Kitchen Nightmares and I figure I’d remember him okay.

In the British edition of Kitchen Nightmares, Gordon changes shirts in front of the camera at least twice per episode, and he doesn’t wear undershirts. In the American edition, people are too busy fighting to learn how to cook, let alone change clothes. Like, Gordon Ramsay should do an evalutation of the American edition of his own damn show and tell the producers that it’s rubbish and then instruct them how to clean up the dramatic music, fire the narrator, and focus on a rustic production with real cooking, or something.

The final “before” drawing is of your hand. Presumably because the face ain’t listening.


One day I will evolve opposable thumbs.

Other than the short thumb, this isn’t really the worst drawing ever. I could have gone back over it with an eraser, if I’d had one, and made the shading all smooth and pretty. Instead I was like “yeah, that’s a reasonable sketch of a hand.”

I timed myself on the last two drawings. I didn’t think of it for the first one. You’re supposed to set aside half an hour for each drawing, and going slower might have helped me, but I’d like to be able to draw my hand faster than I can run a mile. If I’m going to be drawing a lot, I’d like to be efficient.

So far I’m still on the fence on the book’s usefulness. I don’t think I’m its target audience; what I want to learn and what this book tries to teach don’t overlap completely. I think it’s a good refresher for me, as far as observation and concentration are concerned, but I haven’t yet discovered anything that has revolutionized my ways of perceiving. I’ve drawn from life before, not with perfect accuracy but reasonably well, and I’m familiar with viewing objects as a collection of shapes and lines and planes instead of trying to make them fit preconceptions.

However, at this point I’m still a little resistant to the book’s message: it focuses on realism in drawing, and I enjoy drawing with a more cartoony feel (what’s the correct term here? Partial abstraction? I should know this!), and I want the two to inform each other. The author keeps telling us that we need to abandon our symbolic vocabulary. If the symbols don’t work, then yes, throw ‘em out. But unless you draw only from life, you will need symbols. Symbols that are developed through observation, but symbols all the same. I want to be able to draw the same nose from any angle with one or two lines, and you can’t do that without having a comprehensive mental library of working symbols.

I’m guessing this will all come together later, or in a different book with a different focus.

Guard, like, ten-hut or whatever.

By Katt | August 24, 2010

Time for another muggy six hours of marching band practice. You guys brought your sunscreen and thermoses of Mountain Dew, right?

The present participle of “mark time” is “mark timing,” according to everyone else in march banding marching band. That drove me crazy. And of course they’re not doing it right because they’re trying to march in 7.391/4 time and no one bothered to give anyone in the colorguard a copy of the score.

I was the fattest flag girl. Or I thought I was, even though I was nearly identical in size to one of our captains. From the bleachers you couldn’t tell any of us apart anyway. No one’s even looking at you, they’re looking at the helicopter toss you just dropped.

I don’t like the way I drew the two foreground girls in panel two. They shouldn’t be the same height and they shouldn’t be exactly side by side – it looks flat and amateurish. I tried to tilt the shoulders a little bit, but I don’t think it worked out. I need to work on drawing people from the back in general. Teenage Girl turned out pretty good though.

Band is its own little social hierarchy within the larger high school hierarchy. I like how Mandy’s shirt looks like it says “frogs.” Let’s go OHS Frogs!

Ba-duh-duh-dum, ba-duh-duh DOMP DOMP.
