Stop callin’, stop callin’, I don’t wanna think anymore.

By Katt | August 4, 2010

I’ve felt pulled in a lot of different directions lately: I have to improve my drawing and I have to get more comfortable with Photoshop and Illustrator and I need to read everything and I have all these ideas that require me to do all of the above. I’ve decided that, instead of trying to jump into everything at once, I’ll spend a while focusing on pen-and-paper drawing.

Yesterday I said I’d make an effort to draw something every day.  So far I’ve stuck to that, in a letter-of-the-law way.  I’d like to spend an hour a day drawing… but after you put in a day at work, go to the gym, get groceries, and make a quart of ranch dressing, you kinda don’t wanna.  So I’ve been wedging it in little by little, on lunch breaks and on the train.  Half an hour here, a quick study there.

I haven’t done much sketching from life since college. Even then, I never got great at it. I’d get fidgety and distracted after half an hour.

But there’s one thing I really like about it: looking at stuff. That sounds dumb, doesn’t it? I like to create very doodly, cartoony things, and a lot of the things I’m drawn to are similarly doodly and cartoony. If you go too far down that path, you can lose sight of the way real objects and people look.

I’m going to start working through Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain, because it’s frequently recommended and because we already have it in the house. I’d dismissed it as gimmicky before, and the cover is uninspiring (if there’s anything I don’t want to draw, it’s straightforward pencil renderings of someone sitting still), but maybe I’ll learn something anyway.

But the first first thing I need to do is start making more time for it.

Crunchy in milk

By Katt | July 30, 2010

Imaginary products make me giggle.  I’ve got a few fake ads for fake products kicking around in my mind, and I’ll be working on putting them to paper.

I’ve got a few snapshots of some lunch-break doodles:

Here are some kids who love an imaginary breakfast cereal.  Animated cereal commercials always have exactly two kids, a boy and a girl, who rollerblade for the munchy chomp of their preferred cereal.  As I recall, all cereal kids in a given decade look alike.  There could be only one Boy Kid/Girl Kid team out there, and they spend hours every Saturday morning simultaneously chasing after Lucky Charms and keeping their box of Trix away from the rabbit.  They’re greedy, but they’re keeping fit.

And they’ve got to look fit, rather than bloated from simple carbs and refined sugars, but you’ve still got to give them that rounded kid look.  Kids have almost cylindrical-looking torsos.

I’m having trouble getting the look right from memory, though the top center head is close.  Adding irises to the eyes makes a big difference.   I’m going to have to do some research into cereal-kid art styles.  I’ve been putting that off, because it’ll almost certainly entail watching cereal ads until my eyes fall out.

Hiya!

By Katt | July 26, 2010

When I launched this blog, my boyfriend’s reaction was YEAH WIGGLYTUFF POOPIN.  Fair enough, that’s worth getting excited about.  I had a couple other Wigglytuff doodles on that sketchbook page that I really liked but didn’t scan.  In them, he was bug-eyed, sleep-deprived, and generally creepy-looking.  Instead of posting the original drawings, I decided to work on some new, better-realized ones.

Like my previous drawings, these were done in Adobe Ideas.  I still love Ideas, but the inability to save or retrieve colors is frustrating.  Even an eyedropper tool would be an improvement.  To get around this, I worked on all three of these drawings simultaneously, in the same document.  I’d do the bodies for all three, then the shadows for all three, and so on.  From time to time I’d see something that I needed to fix, but didn’t have the color in my palette, so it was a matter of eyeballing it, trying out a little mark, hitting undo, nudging the color selector over a tiny bit, repeating as necessary.  If you look closely you can see a few spots that don’t quite match.

Every medium has advantages and limitations, and you work around them.  This is as true with applications as it is with drawing in charcoal, pencil, or ink.

I could probably send these over to Illustrator and tidy up all the vectors so everything is a little smoother and matchier.

I know I need to work on both my technical skills and my skills of… you know, drawing.  Observational?  I forget what you call it.  In these, I could have done more with shadows.  The top picture isn’t missing much, but the middle and especially the bottom should have shadows.  Getting shadows right was a challenge for me in school, and it’s a challenge now.  When I work with lighter colors, I often hesitate to throw darker colors in the mix, because I don’t think they look right.  I’ll need to practice drawing from life to get a better idea of how to render shadows.

I’m thinking of keeping track of the time I spend working on things.  The idea didn’t occur to me until I was about 80% done with these.  I timed the teeth; they took about two hours.  They were probably the most fun part, too.

Who’s that Pokémon?

By Katt | July 16, 2010

Here is the first of potentially many slightly embarrassing revelations: I really like Pokémon. And not because of nostalgia or camp value. Well, partly because of camp value. The games are well-designed and fun; there’s a lot to do, and they’re easy without being dull or tedious.

And there are all these weird, interesting critters. Not that they’re all brilliant, but I would love to be a fly on the wall at a Pokémon design team meeting. I imagine a scenario where they’re all locked in a room late at, and the boss won’t let them out until they’ve got 30 new Pokémon, and he’s certainly not springing for pizza.

Anyway, they’re fun to draw. I’ve really wanted to enter the Pokémonathon for over a year now. Two things got in my way: first, it would mean drawing a lot of Pokémon I didn’t particularly like, and second, some of those artists in the Pokémonathon are good. I figured I couldn’t measure up.

But I thought about it anyway, and I doodled. I doodled a lot. Here are some of the doodles I like, from maybe a year ago:

Fairly simplistic. I wonder if “drawing everyone with doofy expressions” is a legitimate style.

The Bulbasaur evolutionary line is my favorite. These guys aren’t perfect, but they amuse me:

I also like this Charmander and Charmeleon. If you’ve ever watched the cartoon (okay, fine, I did, but I swear that was purely for camp value), you’ll recall that Ash’s Charmander was kind of a wuss, and then he evolved into a surly Charmeleon. I like to assume that those personalities are species-wide, which I guess makes me a char-ist.

I hate Clefairy. I like Jigglypuff.

One day I’m going to be pitching ideas to some big important client, and they’re going to look up my name and discover that I once drew a picture of Wigglytuff on the toilet, and they will hire someone else.

None of these are Pokémonathon quality, but I may enter at some point – it’d be a good challenge. At any rate, drawing Pokémon is good practice, both for technique and for trying out different styles. I’ve got a few more, which I’ll post soon.

100 Rogues Design-a-Monster

By Katt | July 13, 2010

If you have an iPhone or iPad, check out 100 Rogues.  It’s a great game.  It’s also a really tough game, so it’s good for gamers who appreciate challenge and the concept of impermanence, and a little more frustrating if you like to stay with your character until his stats and inventory are maxed out.  I confess I’m in the latter group, so I haven’t played much of the game.

Nonetheless, I was excited to learn that the creators of 100 Rogues were holding a monster-design contest, and the winning monster would be featured in the sequel.  How awesome is that?

I submitted two monsters.  I came up with the ideas early on, but I’ve been really busy, so I didn’t get around to sending them until the very last day, around 10 pm, from the airport returning from a trip.  Well, 10 pm Mountain Time; I believe they’re on the east coast, so depending on when the precise deadline was, I may have missed it after all.

Both of these are mundane critters.  This was intentional; I figured that wackier designs would run the risk of not really fitting in with the feel of the game.  Instead, I focused on how they would behave in the game: I wanted something unique, but simple and implementable, that the player would find challenging but not annoying.

Here’s the first submission: A Dog!

I suspect the judges are rolling their eyes at this one.  Someone thought a dog was original?  A Dog isn’t the pinnacle of visual innovation, but it does some interesting stuff:

  • By default, A Dog is sleeping.  If the player, or any enemy, comes within a four-tile range of A Dog, it will wake up.  (“Let sleeping dogs lie.”)
  • If awake, A Dog will attack the nearest target, and will continue attacking each target, in order of proximity, until there are no targets left in the room/corridor.  If it cannot see any target, it will go back to sleep.
  • A Dog can be tamed by throwing food at it.  (This might be tricky to implement.)  When tame, it will not attack you, and instead of sleeping when all targets are defeated, it will follow you.

The idea of having a tame dog was inspired by the dog that you get in NetHack, because it was fun to have a dog in that game.  Little did I realize that the meat-throwing thing is exactly how you tame dogs in NetHack. (I’d only ever had the starter dog.)  So instead of being original yet throwbacky, I inadvertently cribbed an old idea.  Man, I wish I’d done the research on that.  Lesson learned.

Maybe my other submission will fare better?  This here is Spearbill.

Spearbill is a large, angry, flightless bird.  Here are its interesting-monster specs:

  • It’s fast!  It moves at double speed.  (Again, not sure how/if this can be implemented.)
  • Because of its speed and agile build, it is difficult to hit with long-range weapons.  However, it is built for agility, not defense: close-range attacks will do a lot of damage.  (This is the opposite of most flying enemies in 100 Rogues, which are easier to hit from a distance.)
  • After killing a Spearbill, the player can occasionally salvage its beak, which makes a good throwing weapon.

Maybe there’s a fast, evasive, beak-dropping bird already in existence, too, but I’m not aware of one.  Spearbill was my first idea, and I like it a little bit better.

I had a third idea that I didn’t end up submitting: a giant kitten made out of stone.  Giant because you cannot get around it, kitten because it is benign and will never attack you, stone because it is invincible.  It wouldn’t be a threat, just an impediment.  However, I decided Giant Stone Kitten would be too frustrating if it managed to get you stuck in a corner.

Both illustrations were done using Adobe Ideas, which is another app you should get.  It’s free, and it’s vector-based, which is so very cool: you draw a line with your finger, and even if it’s a little choppy, the app automatically smooths it into a vector.  So cool.  Its features are limited (you can’t save colors, which drives me nuts), but I love the overall experience – it feels close to pen and paper drawing..  (I plan to get Brushes soon, too, and I’m eager to see how the two apps compare.)
